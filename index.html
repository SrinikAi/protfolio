Srinivas Kachavarpu

AI & Robotics Researcher · Computer Vision · Former Data Scientist
I’m a Swiss-army knife for organizations looking for business value, research-minded, engineering-driven, and comfortable owning the full pipeline from idea to deployment.

I hold a Master’s degree in Digital Technologies and currently work as a Research Assistant at Ostfalia University of Applied Sciences, Germany. My research focuses on computer vision with applications in medical 3D imaging and physical rehabilitation.

During my Bachelor’s in Mechanical Engineering, I had the chance to collaborate with computer science classmates. That experience gradually shifted my interests toward software development, especially data analysis. At the time, not having a formal computer science degree felt like a constraint, and it pushed me to intentionally bridge that gap.

I pursued the Digital Technologies Master’s program, a joint track between Ostfalia University and TU Clausthal, which became a turning point. I went deeper into software engineering, and my curiosity for data led me into big data, machine learning, and deep learning. In parallel, my interest in systems and autonomy drove me into robotics, where I excelled across both worlds.

Throughout my Master’s, I worked on multiple hands-on projects that turned theory into working systems. One defining experience was FireBot (now Warden Robotics), started as a startup incubation effort. I contributed end-to-end: deep learning-based fire detection, autonomous navigation and motion planning, high-level integration of perception and robotics modules, software architecture design, Git submodule setup, and CI/CD with Docker-based deployment.

This work strengthened my problem-solving, analytical thinking, teamwork, independence under pressure, and clear communication of technical ideas. Building a full system raised an important question: how do we deploy such robots reliably at scale? That question motivated independent research and resulted in a novel coverage path planning algorithm, FaReCPP, which was accepted and presented at IROS 2025 in Hangzhou, China.

In parallel, I deepened my research experience in computer vision through contributions to peer-reviewed work, including publications at ISBS and MIUA.

Looking ahead, my interests span foundation models for computer vision, diffusion models, and large language models, with a long-term drive to contribute to the broader vision of Artificial General Intelligence.

Master’s Thesis

Analyzing Temporal Consistency in 2D Human Pose Estimation

I investigated temporal instability in deep learning–based pose estimation on exercise videos, focusing on how frame-by-frame predictions create jitter and reduce clinical usability. I evaluated transformer-based models and introduced lightweight temporal filtering, reducing temporal consistency error by 9% on 90 FPS physiotherapy datasets.
